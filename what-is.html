What is Language Evolution?

Language evolution is the application of evolutionary theory to studying language.

We tend to think of evolution as being mainly a process that affects living things, so it's worth starting with a definition of that.

Biological evolution is standardly defined as change in allele frequencies in populations over time. This change can occur as a result of selection – if members of a population with certain alleles get eaten by predators more often, for example, those alleles will become rarer – but this is not the whole story. Random variation in the alleles that get transmitted between generations can also lead, ultimately, to big changes.

But biological evolution isn't the only kind of evolutionary process. Even if the only question you care about in language evolution is (the huge and very important question) of how we got here – in other words, how we got from being something like a chimp with no language at all to being a species with some 7,000 complex languages – then you still need to take two evolutionary processes into account. The first is biological evolution; the second is cultural evolution.

If biological evolution is change in the frequency of genetic variants in a population over time, then cultural evolution can be seen as change in the frequency of cultural variants. By culture we mean traits that are socially, rather than genetically, transmitted. Natural hair color is genetic in origin; dyed hair is cultural. The fact that humans walk on two legs is genetic; differences in gait are (partly) cultural. Much of language is culturally transmitted, so when people talk about the “cultural evolution of language” they're essentially talking about language change, but viewed as an evolutionary process. Just as biological evolution shaped us to fit our environment; cultural evolution shapes languages to fit us. And, as with biological evolution, cultural evolution can happen as a result of selection, but doesn't have to. Random forces play an important role here too.

Why does the cultural evolution of language matter? There are two reasons. The first is that understanding the biological evolution of the human language capacity may not be enough to understand how we got from there (non-linguistic chimps) to here (humans with thousands of complex languages); there is a growing field of work suggesting that the gap between modern human brains and modern human languages needs to be bridged by culture. Understanding this better is about better understanding what it means to be human. The second reason is that language evolution is happening now, all around us. Understanding that process better is about better understanding how our world works.
